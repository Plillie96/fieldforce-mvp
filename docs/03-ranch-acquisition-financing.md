# 03 — Ranch Acquisition & Financing

Land almost never cash-flows against its own purchase price on cattle income
alone. A working ranch might return 1–3% annually on the land value from
cattle — well below the mortgage rate. That gap is the central problem this
document exists to solve. **The deal has to be structured so the D2C margin,
not the raw cattle operation, services the debt.**

## The core tension

- **Ranch land is priced on scarcity, recreation, and appreciation** — buyers
  bid it up for lifestyle and long-term land value.
- **Cattle income is thin** — the annual operating return doesn't cover a
  full-price mortgage.
- **Therefore:** either buy below "amenity" pricing (on ag-productivity comps),
  reduce the capital at risk (lease part of the operation), or attach a
  higher-margin business (D2C beef) to the same land. We do the third, and try
  for the first.

## Financing paths (usually a blend)

### 1. Equity
The founder capital. Ag lenders typically want **25–50% down** on farm/ranch
real estate — more than a house. This is the single biggest gate.

### 2. USDA Farm Service Agency (FSA) loans
Purpose-built for exactly this buyer.
- **Farm Ownership Loans** — up to a program cap toward land purchase; longer
  amortization than commercial ag loans.
- **Beginning Farmer & Rancher** provisions — lower down payment, and
  **down-payment loan programs** that pair with a commercial lender.
- **Microloans** for smaller operations/startup costs.
- Requires a business plan and typically some farming experience or a mentor —
  which is another reason to prototype the operation first (doc 04).

### 3. Farm Credit System / ag banks
Farm Credit institutions and ag-focused banks (and Federal Land Bank–lineage
lenders) underwrite ranch real estate that a normal mortgage bank won't touch.
Rates and terms are ag-specific.

### 4. Seller financing
Common in ranch deals, especially with retiring owners. Seller carries part of
the note. Often more flexible on down payment and can bridge a gap the bank
won't. **Actively ask** — many sellers prefer the installment tax treatment.

### 5. Lease-to-own / long-term grazing lease
The asset-light entry. Lease the ground (or graze on shares) to run cattle and
prove the operation before buying, sometimes with a purchase option. Preserves
capital and de-risks the location.

### 6. Complementary / non-cattle income to service debt
Underwrite the deal knowing land can also earn from:
- Hunting / recreational leases
- Conservation programs (USDA EQIP/CRP, carbon and grassland easements)
- Agritourism, events, cabin/short-term rental
- Hay sales, breeding-stock sales
These aren't the point of the business, but they can be the difference between
a deal that services its debt and one that doesn't.

## Deal structure the model assumes

Against the assumed **$2.0M capital** and a working ranch:

- Buy a property whose land value the cattle+DTC operation can plausibly carry.
- ~30–40% down from equity; balance financed via FSA/Farm Credit blend.
- Reserve a meaningful slug of the $2.0M as **working capital** — cattle,
  processing prepayment, brand launch, and 12–18 months of operating runway.
  Do **not** sink all capital into the down payment; a ranch with no operating
  cash is a fast failure.

> Rule of thumb the model uses: **no more than ~60–65% of total capital into
> the land down payment; ≥35% held for herd + operations + runway.**

## Due diligence before closing (non-negotiable)

- [ ] Title search + title insurance; confirm access and easements
- [ ] Water rights: documented, quantified, transferable (get a water attorney
      in prior-appropriation states)
- [ ] Mineral rights status and any active leases
- [ ] Survey / acreage verification; fence-line vs. deed-line disputes
- [ ] Environmental / Phase I where warranted (old fuel tanks, dumping)
- [ ] Existing leases (grazing, hunting) — do they convey or terminate?
- [ ] Property tax basis and any ag-use valuation that could reset on sale
- [ ] Independent range/forage assessment of true carrying capacity
- [ ] Home & structures inspection

## The financing critical path

1. Get pre-qualified with an ag lender / FSA **before** making offers — know the
   real budget.
2. Build the business plan (this repo) — lenders and FSA require it.
3. Prototype demand (doc 04) to strengthen the loan application and de-risk.
4. Make offers contingent on financing, water-rights verification, and
   carrying-capacity assessment.
