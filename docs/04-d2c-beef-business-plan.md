# 04 — D2C Beef Business Plan

This is where the returns come from. The ranch secures supply and the balance
sheet; the direct-to-consumer brand is what earns above-commodity margins.

## Product

Grass-fed, grass-finished beef sold direct to households. Core SKUs:

- **Curated boxes** — mixed cuts (ground, roasts, steaks) at set weights/prices.
  The workhorse product and the subscription anchor.
- **Bulk shares** — quarter / half / whole animal. Huge single orders, fill the
  freezer, and are the most capital-efficient way to move a whole carcass
  (you sell the *entire* animal, not just the premium cuts).
- **Premium à la carte** — steaks, dry-aged selections at the top of the range.
- **Nose-to-tail add-ons** — bones, tallow, organ blends, jerky. These monetize
  the parts that would otherwise be waste and lift the value per carcass.

### The whole-carcass problem (design the menu around it)
An animal is not all ribeyes. A steer yields a fixed ratio of ground, roasts,
and premium steaks. If you only sell steaks, you're stuck with a freezer of
ground beef. **The product mix and pricing must move the *entire* carcass** —
which is exactly why bulk shares, subscription boxes, and nose-to-tail SKUs
matter more than they first appear.

## Positioning & brand

- **Provenance is the product.** One ranch, named animals-to-land traceability,
  a real family/place story. This is what a grocery chain and even ButcherBox
  (which aggregates many suppliers) can't authentically claim.
- **Defensible claims only.** Grass-finished, no added hormones, humanely
  raised, regenerative practices — each backed by verifiable practice and, where
  worthwhile, third-party certification (doc 06).
- Premium but honest pricing; the customer is buying trust and taste, not the
  cheapest protein.

## Channels

1. **Owned e-commerce (primary).** Shopify + a subscription app. Own the
   customer relationship and the data; no marketplace taking a cut of every order.
2. **Subscription (the core economic engine).** Recurring monthly boxes convert
   a one-time buyer into predictable revenue and smooth demand against a herd
   that finishes on a schedule.
3. **Local / farm-direct.** On-ranch sales, farmers' markets, local restaurants —
   higher margin, builds the origin story, and (depending on state rules) can use
   lighter-weight processing paths (doc 06).
4. **Wholesale / retail (later).** Only after the brand and supply are proven.

## Fulfillment & logistics

Beef ships **frozen**, which is operationally demanding:
- Insulated boxes + dry ice or gel packs; 2-day (or faster) shipping so it
  arrives frozen.
- Shipping is a major, often underestimated cost line — model it explicitly per
  box (doc 05), and use it to justify subscription minimums and free-shipping
  thresholds that push larger orders.
- Consider regional carrier zones and a fulfillment location near the processor.

## Operating model (phased)

### Phase A — Validate demand *before* the land closes
Sell beef sourced from a **partner ranch** under our brand. Stand up the store,
run the subscription, ship real boxes, and measure: conversion, repeat rate,
CAC, shipping cost reality, and product mix. This de-risks the entire venture
and strengthens the loan application — for a fraction of the capital.

### Phase B — Own supply
Once the ranch is acquired and the first cattle finish, transition supply from
partner-sourced to our own herd. Contract with a USDA-inspected processor
(don't build a plant yet).

### Phase C — Scale
Grow subscribers to match herd throughput; add SKUs; evaluate owned processing
or a second supply source only when demand exceeds one ranch's output.

## The metrics that decide whether this works

- **Contribution margin per box** (after product COGS, processing, packaging,
  shipping, payment fees) — must be positive and healthy.
- **Value captured per carcass** — total DTC revenue from one animal vs. its
  commodity auction price. This is the whole thesis in one number.
- **Repeat / subscription retention rate** — the difference between a business
  and a treadmill.
- **CAC vs. LTV** — can we acquire a customer for less than they're worth?
- **Sell-through by cut** — are we actually moving the whole carcass, or
  drowning in ground beef?
