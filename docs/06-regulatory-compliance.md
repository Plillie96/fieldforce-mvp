# 06 — Regulatory & Compliance

Meat is one of the most heavily regulated consumer products in the U.S. The
rules here are not red tape to route around — **they determine which sales
channels are even legal**, and getting them wrong can shut the business down.
This document is an orientation, **not legal advice**; confirm everything with a
food/ag attorney and your state Department of Agriculture before selling.

## The core rule: how the animal was slaughtered decides who you can sell to

There are three inspection paths, and they gate the market:

| Path | What it allows | Constraint |
|------|----------------|-----------|
| **USDA (federal) inspection** | Sell **anywhere, including across state lines** and online DTC | Must use a **USDA-FSIS-inspected** slaughter/processing facility |
| **State inspection** | Sell **within the same state** | Only in states with an FSIS-approved state program; no interstate |
| **Custom-exempt ("custom slaughter")** | Only to the person(s) who **already own the live animal** | The classic **"cow share" / bulk-share** model — buyer purchases the live animal (or a share) *before* slaughter |

### Why this shapes the whole channel strategy
- To ship curated boxes DTC across the country (doc 04's primary channel), the
  beef **must be USDA-inspected.** This is non-negotiable for e-commerce.
- The **bulk-share ("buy a quarter/half/whole cow")** model can use a
  **custom-exempt** processor because the customer legally owns the live animal
  before slaughter — a lighter-weight, lower-cost path that's great for
  local/regional sales and early revenue.
- **Processor access is the real bottleneck.** USDA-inspected small-plant
  capacity is scarce and often booked 6–12+ months out. **Secure processing
  slots early** — it can gate your entire launch timeline. Verify this during
  ranch search (proximity to a USDA plant is a scored criterion in doc 02).

## Labeling & marketing claims (each is legally regulated)

- **"Grass-fed" / "grass-finished"** — claims are scrutinized; the former USDA
  grass-fed standard was withdrawn, so substantiation and third-party
  certification (e.g., **American Grassfed Association**) now carry the weight.
  Say only what you can prove.
- **"Organic"** — a certified USDA term; requires certification. Don't use it
  loosely.
- **"No hormones added" / "raised without antibiotics"** — permitted only with
  documentation supporting the claim.
- **"Natural," "humane," "regenerative"** — varying definitions; "humane" and
  "regenerative" often lean on third-party certifiers (Certified Humane, Global
  Animal Partnership, etc.). Back every claim.
- **Label approval** — USDA-inspected product labels generally require FSIS
  review/approval. Nutrition and safe-handling requirements apply.

## Business & operational compliance

- [ ] Business entity + ag/business licenses; state Dept. of Agriculture
      registration for meat sales
- [ ] Food safety: HACCP plan lives with the processor; understand your
      responsibilities for storage, handling, cold chain, and recalls
- [ ] Cold-chain integrity in fulfillment (frozen, documented)
- [ ] Sales tax / nexus for interstate e-commerce
- [ ] Product liability insurance (food) + farm/ranch liability
- [ ] Animal ID / traceability (brand registration, tags) per state
- [ ] Water discharge / CAFO thresholds (mostly irrelevant at pasture-based
      scale, but know the line), and any local nuisance/zoning rules for on-farm
      sales and agritourism

## Practical compliance sequence

1. **Confirm the processing path** for each channel you plan to use — USDA for
   shipped boxes, custom-exempt acceptable for local bulk shares.
2. **Lock processor capacity** at a USDA-inspected plant before committing to a
   launch date.
3. **Choose claims early** and align ranch practices to substantiate them (a
   grass-finished claim constrains how you raise every animal).
4. **Get labels reviewed** before printing.
5. **Have a food/ag attorney** review the model, claims, and contracts.

> Bottom line: the legal path is a **design input, not an afterthought.** The
> business plan (doc 04) and financial model (doc 05) already assume USDA
> contract processing for shipped DTC and allow custom-exempt for local bulk
> shares — because the regulations require it.
